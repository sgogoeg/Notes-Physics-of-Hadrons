\section{Groups, Isospin and Colour}
\label{sec:groups}

The proliferation of strongly interacting particles catalogued in the
``particle zoo'' (Sect.~\ref{sec:introduction}) demanded an organising principle
analogous to the periodic table, in which a recurring ordering scheme betrays an
underlying substructure. The decisive insight, due to Gell-Mann and Ne'eman, was
that the observed hadrons populate \emph{multiplets} which are the irreducible
representations of a continuous symmetry group, $\mathrm{SU}(3)_f$, and that these
multiplets are naturally built from a fundamental triplet of constituents---the
quarks \cite[Ch.~1]{Amsler2018}. This chapter develops the group-theoretical
machinery underlying that classification. It begins with the abstract notion of a
symmetry group, specialises to the isospin group $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, shows how
representations are combined, extends the formalism to the flavour group
$\mathrm{SU}(3)$, introduces Young tableaux as a systematic bookkeeping tool, and
closes with the exact colour symmetry $\mathrm{SU}(3)_c$ that underpins Quantum
Chromodynamics.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Symmetries, groups and Lie algebras}
\label{subsec:symmetries}

A symmetry is a transformation that leaves the physics of a system unchanged, and
the set of all such transformations forms a \emph{group}. The rotation group serves
as the prototype. Two successive rotations $R_1$ and $R_2$ compose into a single
rotation $R_3 = R_2 \cdot R_1$, so the set of rotations is closed under composition;
the identity (no rotation) belongs to the set, every rotation possesses an inverse,
and composition is associative. Rotations about different axes do not in general
commute, $R_1 \cdot R_2 \neq R_2 \cdot R_1$, so the group is non-Abelian. Because a
rotation depends continuously and differentiably on its parameters (the rotation
angles), the rotation group is a \emph{Lie group}, whose defining feature is that
any finite element can be reached as a succession of infinitesimal transformations
close to the identity \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}.

The physical requirement that no measurement depend on the orientation of the
apparatus fixes the way symmetry transformations act on quantum states. If a
rotation maps $\ket{\Psi} \to \ket{\Psi'} = U\ket{\Psi}$, the transition probability
between two states must be preserved,
\begin{equation}
    |\braket{\Phi|\Psi}|^2
    = |\braket{\Phi'|\Psi'}|^2
    = |\bra{\Phi} U^\dagger U \ket{\Psi}|^2,
\end{equation}
which forces $U$ to be unitary, $U^\dagger U = \mathbb{1}$. Demanding in addition
that matrix elements of the Hamiltonian be preserved leads to
\begin{equation}
    H = U^\dagger H\, U
    \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
    [\,U, H\,] = 0 ,
\end{equation}
the statement that a symmetry of the dynamics is equivalent to the existence of an
operator commuting with $H$. For a time-independent transformation the equation of
motion $i\,\mathrm{d}\ket{\Psi}/\mathrm{d}t = H\ket{\Psi}$ then implies that the
expectation value of $U$ is a constant of the motion, so each continuous symmetry
yields a conserved quantity \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}.

The local structure of a Lie group is encoded in its behaviour near the identity. An
infinitesimal rotation through an angle $\epsilon$ about the $z$-axis is written
\begin{equation}
    U = \mathbb{1} - i\,\epsilon\, J_3 ,
    \label{eq:infinitesimal}
\end{equation}
where the Hermitian operator $J_3$ is the \emph{generator} of the rotation. Working
out the action of an infinitesimal spatial rotation on a wavefunction
$\Psi(\vec{r})$ and expanding to first order identifies the generator with the third
component of the orbital angular momentum operator,
\begin{equation}
    J_3 = x\,p_y - y\,p_x ,
\end{equation}
so that invariance under rotations is equivalent to conservation of angular
momentum \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}. A finite rotation is recovered by exponentiating
$N$ infinitesimal ones,
\begin{equation}
    U(\Theta) = \lim_{N\to\infty}\!\left(\mathbb{1} - i\,\frac{\Theta}{N}\,J_3\right)^{\!N}
    = e^{-i\,\Theta J_3}.
\end{equation}

The three generators $J_1, J_2, J_3$ of rotations about the coordinate axes do not
commute; instead they close under commutation into a \emph{Lie algebra},
\begin{equation}
    [\,J_j, J_k\,] = i\,\epsilon_{jkl}\,J_l ,
    \label{eq:su2algebra}
\end{equation}
where the totally antisymmetric symbol $\epsilon_{jkl}$ furnishes the
\emph{structure constants} of the group, equal to $+1$ for a cyclic permutation of
$jkl$, $-1$ for an anti-cyclic one, and zero otherwise. The commutation relations
define the group properties independently of any particular representation. Because
the generators do not commute, the eigenvalues of only one of them, conventionally
$J_3$, can be used as a good quantum number; the Casimir operator $J^2$ commutes
with all three generators, $[\,J^2, J_i\,] = 0$, and the simultaneous eigenstates
$\ket{jm}$ satisfy
\begin{equation}
    J^2 \ket{jm} = j(j+1)\ket{jm},
    \qquad
    J_3 \ket{jm} = m\ket{jm}.
\end{equation}
This algebraic structure is common to spin, isospin and indeed any angular-momentum
variable, and it is fulfilled for every allowed value $j = 0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1,
\tfrac{3}{2}, \dots$ \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{SU(2): Spin and isospin}
\label{subsec:su2}

The lowest-dimensional non-trivial representation of the rotation algebra
\eqref{eq:su2algebra} is the spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ representation, in which the
generators are represented by the Pauli matrices,
\begin{equation}
    J_i = \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_i ,
    \qquad
    \sigma_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix},\;
    \sigma_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix},\;
    \sigma_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
acting on the two basis spinors $\binom{1}{0}$ for $m = +\tfrac{1}{2}$ and
$\binom{0}{1}$ for $m = -\tfrac{1}{2}$. The corresponding transformation matrices
are
\begin{equation}
    U(\Theta_i) = e^{-i\,\Theta_i \sigma_i/2}
    = \mathbb{1}\cos\tfrac{\Theta_i}{2} - i\,\sigma_i \sin\tfrac{\Theta_i}{2},
\end{equation}
which are unitary because the $\sigma_i$ are Hermitian. Since the Pauli matrices are
traceless, the identity $\det\!\big(e^{i\sigma}\big) = e^{i\,\mathrm{Tr}(\sigma)} =
1$ shows that these matrices have unit determinant. The set of all such matrices
therefore forms the \emph{special unitary group in two dimensions},
$\mathrm{SU}(2)$, whose two-dimensional representation is the fundamental one; from
it the representations of dimension $1, 2, 3, 4, \dots$, corresponding to $j = 0,
\tfrac{1}{2}, 1, \tfrac{3}{2}, \dots$, can be constructed \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}.

The same mathematics applies, in an entirely different physical context, to
\emph{isospin}. The concept was introduced by Heisenberg in 1932 to express the
near-equality of the proton and neutron masses ($m_p = 938.3$~MeV, $m_n =
939.6$~MeV) and the charge independence of the nuclear force. In analogy with spin,
one regards the nucleon as a single particle $N$ with isospin $i = \tfrac{1}{2}$
existing in two charge sub-states distinguished by the third component $i_3$,
\begin{equation}
    \ket{p} = \ket{\tfrac{1}{2}, +\tfrac{1}{2}},
    \qquad
    \ket{n} = \ket{\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{2}}.
\end{equation}
Just as the $2s+1$ spin states of a particle are degenerate in the absence of
external fields, the $2i+1$ members of an isospin multiplet have nearly equal masses
in the absence of electromagnetism. Isospin is a useful concept precisely because it
is conserved in strong interactions: the strong force depends on the total isospin
$I$ but not on its projection $I_3$ \cite[Ch.~3]{Amsler2018}.

The electromagnetic interaction breaks this symmetry. The electric charge is fixed
not by $I_3$ alone but by the Gell-Mann--Nishijima relation,
\begin{equation}
    Q = I_3 + \frac{Y}{2},
    \qquad
    Y = B + S,
    \label{eq:gmn}
\end{equation}
where $Y$ is the hypercharge, $B$ the baryon number and $S$ the strangeness. Because
the photon couples to $Q$, the electromagnetic interaction singles out the $I_3$
component and lifts the degeneracy within a multiplet \cite[Ch.~3]{Amsler2018}. With
the help of \eqref{eq:gmn} the $i_3$ values are assigned to the members of each
multiplet, for example $\pi^+, \pi^0, \pi^-$ for the pion triplet ($i = 1$) and
$\big(p, n\big)$ for the nucleon doublet. Since $i_3$ is additive and the isospin of
a hadron is the vector sum of the constituent isospins, the up and down quarks of
the first generation form an isospin doublet with $i_3(u) = +\tfrac{1}{2}$ and
$i_3(d) = -\tfrac{1}{2}$, while the strange quark is an isospin singlet
\cite[Ch.~3]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Addition of spin}
\label{subsec:addition}

Two systems carrying angular momenta $j_A$ and $j_B$ can be described in the product
basis $\ket{j_A m_A}\ket{j_B m_B}$. The total angular momentum $\vec{J} = \vec{J}_A +
\vec{J}_B$ again satisfies the Lie algebra \eqref{eq:su2algebra}, and the product of
the two representations, of dimensions $(2j_A+1)$ and $(2j_B+1)$, decomposes into a
direct sum of irreducible representations with
\begin{equation}
    J = |j_A - j_B|,\; |j_A - j_B| + 1,\; \dots,\; j_A + j_B .
\end{equation}
The eigenstates of total angular momentum are linear combinations of the product
states,
\begin{equation}
    \ket{j_A j_B\, J M} = \sum_{m_A, m_B} C(m_A m_B; J M)\,
    \ket{j_A m_A}\ket{j_B m_B},
    \qquad M = m_A + m_B,
\end{equation}
where the expansion coefficients $C(m_A m_B; J M)$ are the Clebsch--Gordan
coefficients \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}. For two spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ systems this
reads symbolically
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} = \mathbf{3} \oplus \mathbf{1},
    \label{eq:2x2}
\end{equation}
the dimension of each irreducible representation being equal to the dimension of the
corresponding multiplet. The symmetric triplet and the antisymmetric singlet of two
spins are explicitly
\begin{align}
    \ket{1,+1} &= \,\uparrow\uparrow, &
    \ket{1,0} &= \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\uparrow\downarrow + \downarrow\uparrow), &
    \ket{1,-1} &= \,\downarrow\downarrow, \\
    \ket{0,0} &= \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\uparrow\downarrow - \downarrow\uparrow), & & & &
\end{align}
and the analogous decomposition of the two-nucleon isospin states replaces
$\uparrow$ by $p$ and $\downarrow$ by $n$.

A classic application is the determination of the deuteron isospin. The total
non-relativistic wavefunction of the two-nucleon system factorises into space, spin
and isospin parts,
\begin{equation}
    \Psi_{\mathrm{total}} = \phi(\mathrm{space}) \cdot \alpha(\mathrm{spin})
    \cdot \chi(\mathrm{isospin}),
\end{equation}
and must be antisymmetric under exchange of the two identical fermions. The deuteron
has spin $1$, so its spin function $\alpha$ is symmetric; its spatial wavefunction is
dominantly an $\ell = 0$ $S$-wave, which is symmetric since it transforms as
$(-1)^\ell$ under interchange. Antisymmetry of the total wavefunction therefore
requires the isospin function $\chi$ to be antisymmetric, which selects the singlet:
the deuteron has $I = 0$ \cite[Ch.~3]{Amsler2018}. This assignment is confirmed by
comparing the cross sections of the two reactions
\begin{equation}
    \text{(a)}\;\; p + p \to d + \pi^+,
    \qquad
    \text{(b)}\;\; p + n \to d + \pi^0,
\end{equation}
whose initial states have isospin $1$ and a mixture of $0$ and $1$ respectively. Only
the $I = 1$ component contributes to deuteron production, and isospin symmetry then
predicts the ratio of cross sections
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\sigma(\text{a})}{\sigma(\text{b})} = \frac{2}{1},
\end{equation}
in agreement with experiment \cite[Ch.~3]{Amsler2018}.

The same isospin bookkeeping accounts for the relative strengths of pion--nucleon
scattering. The $\pi N$ system couples to $i = \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $i = \tfrac{3}{2}$,
and at low energy the cross sections are dominated by the excitation of the
$\Delta(1232)$ resonance with $i = \tfrac{3}{2}$. Isospin conservation then fixes the
maximal total cross sections to be roughly in the ratio
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{\mathrm{tot}}(\pi^+ p) : \sigma_{\mathrm{tot}}(\pi^- p) \approx 3 : 1 ,
\end{equation}
consistent with the measured peak values of about $210$~mb and $70$~mb, respectively
\cite[Ch.~3]{Amsler2018}.

The procedure extends to more than two constituents by iterating the decomposition.
For three spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ systems one finds
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2}
    = (\mathbf{3} \oplus \mathbf{1}) \otimes \mathbf{2}
    = \mathbf{4} \oplus \mathbf{2} \oplus \mathbf{2},
    \label{eq:2x2x2}
\end{equation}
giving one quadruplet and two doublets. While such repeated coupling with
Clebsch--Gordan coefficients is feasible for $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, it becomes unwieldy
for the larger groups encountered below; a more systematic graphical tool, the Young
tableaux, is developed in Sect.~\ref{subsec:youngtableaux}.

\subsection{Isospin of antiparticles}
\label{subsec:antiparticles}

The treatment of antiparticles within $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ requires care. From the
charge assignments and the Gell-Mann--Nishijima relation one reads off the isospin
projections
\begin{equation}
    p : \ket{\tfrac{1}{2}, +\tfrac{1}{2}}, \quad
    n : \ket{\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{2}}, \quad
    \bar{p} : \ket{\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{2}}, \quad
    \bar{n} : \ket{\tfrac{1}{2}, +\tfrac{1}{2}} .
\end{equation}
It is tempting to group the antinucleons into the doublet $\binom{\bar{n}}{\bar{p}}$,
in parallel with $\binom{p}{n}$, but this naive assignment is wrong: particles and
antiparticles must transform in the same way under isospin rotations if the same
Clebsch--Gordan coefficients are to be used for both \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}.

Consider a rotation through an angle $\Theta$ about the $2$-axis in isospin space,
generated by the Pauli matrix $\tau_2$,
\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix} p' \\ n' \end{pmatrix}
    = e^{-i\Theta\tau_2/2}
    \begin{pmatrix} p \\ n \end{pmatrix}
    = \begin{pmatrix}
        \cos\tfrac{\Theta}{2} & -\sin\tfrac{\Theta}{2} \\[2pt]
        \sin\tfrac{\Theta}{2} & \;\cos\tfrac{\Theta}{2}
      \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix} p \\ n \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
so that $p' = p\cos\tfrac{\Theta}{2} - n\sin\tfrac{\Theta}{2}$ and $n' =
p\sin\tfrac{\Theta}{2} + n\cos\tfrac{\Theta}{2}$. For the antiparticles to transform
identically one requires
\begin{equation}
    \bar{p}' = \bar{p}\cos\tfrac{\Theta}{2} - \bar{n}\sin\tfrac{\Theta}{2},
    \qquad
    \bar{n}' = \bar{p}\sin\tfrac{\Theta}{2} + \bar{n}\cos\tfrac{\Theta}{2}.
\end{equation}
These relations can be cast in exactly the same matrix form as for the particles
provided the antiparticle doublet is ordered with a relative sign,
\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix} -\bar{n}' \\ \bar{p}' \end{pmatrix}
    = \begin{pmatrix}
        \cos\tfrac{\Theta}{2} & -\sin\tfrac{\Theta}{2} \\[2pt]
        \sin\tfrac{\Theta}{2} & \;\cos\tfrac{\Theta}{2}
      \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix} -\bar{n} \\ \bar{p} \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The correct antinucleon doublet is therefore $\binom{-\bar{n}}{\bar{p}}$, and the
corresponding antiquark doublet is $\binom{-\bar{d}}{\bar{u}}$. The lesson is that in
$\mathrm{SU}(2)$ a representation and its complex conjugate have identical
transformation properties; the doublet $\mathbf{2}$ and the antidoublet
$\mathbf{2}^*$ are equivalent \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}. This pseudoreality is special
to $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ and does not carry over to $\mathrm{SU}(3)$, where the
fundamental triplet $\mathbf{3}$ and its conjugate $\mathbf{3}^*$ are genuinely
distinct, as discussed next.

\subsection{SU(3): Gell-Mann's Eightfold Way}
\label{subsec:su3}

The need to enlarge the symmetry group arose from the discovery of strange
particles. Hadrons such as the $\Lambda$ and the $K^0$ are copiously produced in
strong interactions, for example in $\pi^- p \to \Lambda K^0$, yet decay only weakly
and hence with comparatively long lifetimes ($\tau_\Lambda = 2.6 \times
10^{-10}$~s); moreover, they are always produced in pairs. To account for this
behaviour Gell-Mann (1953) and Nishijima (1955) introduced an additive quantum
number, the \emph{strangeness} $S$, conserved by the strong but not the weak
interaction \cite[Ch.~1]{Amsler2018}. Strangeness enters the Gell-Mann--Nishijima
relation \eqref{eq:gmn} through the hypercharge $Y = B + S$, and the crucial
observation is that the hypercharge operator $Y$ can be diagonalised simultaneously
with $I_3$. Since a special unitary group $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ admits $N-1$ mutually
commuting generators, accommodating the two simultaneously diagonal operators $I_3$
and $Y$ requires the rank-two group $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ \cite[Ch.~7]{Amsler2018}.

The group $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ consists of the unitary $3 \times 3$ matrices with unit
determinant, generated by the eight linearly independent traceless Hermitian
matrices $G_i = \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda_i$, where the $\lambda_i$ are the Gell-Mann
matrices,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \lambda_1 &= \begin{pmatrix} 0&1&0\\1&0&0\\0&0&0 \end{pmatrix},
    & \lambda_2 &= \begin{pmatrix} 0&-i&0\\i&0&0\\0&0&0 \end{pmatrix},
    & \lambda_3 &= \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&0\\0&-1&0\\0&0&0 \end{pmatrix}, \\[4pt]
    \lambda_4 &= \begin{pmatrix} 0&0&1\\0&0&0\\1&0&0 \end{pmatrix},
    & \lambda_5 &= \begin{pmatrix} 0&0&-i\\0&0&0\\i&0&0 \end{pmatrix},
    & & \\[4pt]
    \lambda_6 &= \begin{pmatrix} 0&0&0\\0&0&1\\0&1&0 \end{pmatrix},
    & \lambda_7 &= \begin{pmatrix} 0&0&0\\0&0&-i\\0&i&0 \end{pmatrix},
    & \lambda_8 &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\begin{pmatrix} 1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&-2 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:gellmann}
\end{equation}
These replace the Pauli matrices of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ and obey the Lie algebra
\begin{equation}
    \big[\,\tfrac{1}{2}\lambda_r, \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda_s\,\big]
    = i\,f_{rsk}\,\tfrac{1}{2}\lambda_k ,
\end{equation}
with the totally antisymmetric structure constants $f_{rsk}$ \cite[Ch.~7]{Amsler2018}.
Two of the generators are diagonal and are identified with the physical quantum
numbers
\begin{equation}
    I_3 = \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda_3,
    \qquad
    Y = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\lambda_8 .
\end{equation}
Because the $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ submatrices reappear within $\lambda_{1,2}$, in
$\lambda_{4,5}$, and in $\lambda_{6,7}$, the group $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ contains three
$\mathrm{SU}(2)$ subgroups---the familiar $I$-spin together with the $V$-spin and
$U$-spin---whose ladder operators are
\begin{equation}
    I_\pm = G_1 \pm iG_2,
    \qquad
    V_\pm = G_4 \pm iG_5,
    \qquad
    U_\pm = G_6 \pm iG_7 .
\end{equation}

The fundamental representation is three-dimensional, and its basis states are the
light quarks
\begin{equation}
    u = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
    d = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
    s = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
which are eigenstates of the diagonal operators $I_3$ and $Y$. Acting with the
diagonal Gell-Mann matrices,
\begin{equation}
    I_3\,\Phi = \tfrac{1}{2}\!
    \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&0\\0&-1&0\\0&0&0 \end{pmatrix}\!
    \begin{pmatrix} u\\d\\s \end{pmatrix}
    = \begin{pmatrix} +\tfrac{1}{2}\,u\\ -\tfrac{1}{2}\,d\\ 0 \end{pmatrix},
    \qquad
    Y\,\Phi = \tfrac{1}{3}\!
    \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&-2 \end{pmatrix}\!
    \begin{pmatrix} u\\d\\s \end{pmatrix}
    = \begin{pmatrix} +\tfrac{1}{3}\,u\\ +\tfrac{1}{3}\,d\\ -\tfrac{2}{3}\,s \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
reproduces, via \eqref{eq:gmn}, the complete set of quark quantum numbers collected
in Table~\ref{tab:quarks} \cite[Ch.~7]{Amsler2018}.

Antiquarks transform under the conjugate representation $\mathbf{3}^*$, whose weight
diagram is the inversion of the triplet through the origin. Unlike the
$\mathrm{SU}(2)$ case discussed in Sect.~\ref{subsec:antiparticles}, the triplet and
antitriplet of $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ are inequivalent. Hadrons are then built as
$\mathrm{SU}(3)$ multiplets from these fundamental objects: mesons as quark--antiquark
states $q\bar{q}$ and baryons as three-quark states $qqq$. The systematic
construction of the resulting multiplets is the subject of the next subsection.

% ============================================================
\subsection{SU(N) multiplets and Young tableaux}
\label{subsec:youngtableaux}

To find the multiplet content of a product of representations one needs the
dimensions and permutation symmetries of the irreducible representations contained
in it. The \emph{Young tableaux} provide a graphical algorithm that accomplishes
this for $\mathrm{SU}(N)$, the relevant decompositions for the physics of hadrons
being
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{3} \otimes \mathbf{3}^* = \mathbf{8} \oplus \mathbf{1},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{3} \otimes \mathbf{3} \otimes \mathbf{3}
    = \mathbf{10} \oplus \mathbf{8} \oplus \mathbf{8} \oplus \mathbf{1},
    \label{eq:su3decomp}
\end{equation}
for the $q\bar q$ mesons and $qqq$ baryons, respectively
\cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}. The symmetry of the wavefunctions plays a decisive role:
the total meson wavefunction must have a definite symmetry, whereas the total baryon
wavefunction must be antisymmetric because it describes three identical fermions.

In the tableau formalism a single box represents the fundamental representation of
dimension $N$, while a single column of $N-1$ boxes represents the conjugate
representation $N^*$. A valid tableau must be left- and top-justified. The dimension
of a tableau is obtained as a ratio of two products. For the numerator one inserts
the value $N$ in the top-left box, adds one unit when moving to the right and
subtracts one unit when descending, so that the box in row $r$ and column $c$ carries
the value $N - r + c$; the numerator is the product of all these values. For the
denominator one computes, for each box, its \emph{hook}---the number of boxes met by
a path entering from the right and exiting at the bottom of that box---and multiplies
the hooks together. The dimension is the product of values divided by the product of
hooks \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}.

As a first illustration, the coupling of two fundamental doublets of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$
is represented by the product of two single boxes, which decomposes into a
horizontal pair of boxes (the symmetric representation) and a vertical pair (the
antisymmetric one),
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} = \mathbf{3} \oplus \mathbf{1},
\end{equation}
recovering the spin triplet and singlet of Eq.~\eqref{eq:2x2}. The identical box
structure applied to $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ instead yields $\mathbf{3} \otimes \mathbf{3} =
\mathbf{6} \oplus \mathbf{3}^*$, since the antisymmetric vertical pair of boxes is
the conjugate triplet in $\mathrm{SU}(3)$. The geometry of the boxes thus encodes the
permutation symmetry directly: a row of boxes is totally symmetric, a column totally
antisymmetric.

Higher products are evaluated by combining tableaux according to a definite recipe.
One labels the boxes of the second tableau row by row with a sequence of symbols
$a, b, c, \dots$, then appends them to the first tableau from the right: the $a$'s
are added so that no column contains more than one $a$; the $b$'s are added next,
subject to the constraint that, reading from right to left and top to bottom, the
running count of $b$'s never exceeds that of $a$'s; the procedure is repeated for the
$c$'s, after which the dimension of each resulting tableau is computed as above
\cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}. Applying this to three fundamental triplets reproduces the
baryon decomposition of Eq.~\eqref{eq:su3decomp},
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{3} \otimes \mathbf{3} \otimes \mathbf{3}
    = \mathbf{10}_S \oplus \mathbf{8}_{MS} \oplus \mathbf{8}_{MA} \oplus \mathbf{1}_A ,
\end{equation}
in which the subscripts label the totally symmetric decuplet, the two mixed-symmetry
octets, and the totally antisymmetric singlet. Each irreducible representation of
$\mathrm{SU}(3)$ can be labelled by a pair of integers $(\alpha, \beta)$, the numbers
of unpaired boxes below the first and second rows of its tableau, which fixes the
shape of the corresponding weight diagram in the $(I_3, Y)$-plane and distinguishes,
for instance, the decuplet $\mathbf{10} = (3,0)$ from its conjugate $\mathbf{10}^* =
(0,3)$ \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}.

A word of caution applies to the $q\bar q$ mesons. Because a meson is composed of a
quark in the $\mathbf{3}$ and an antiquark in the $\mathbf{3}^*$, the row-versus-column
symmetry of the Young tableau is not directly meaningful, and the symmetry of the
meson wavefunctions must be analysed separately. For baryons, by contrast, the totally
antisymmetric requirement on the three-fermion wavefunction cannot be met by the
flavour, spin and spatial degrees of freedom alone---the ground-state baryons sit in
symmetric flavour--spin combinations with $\ell = 0$---which is the puzzle resolved by
the introduction of colour \cite[Ch.~6]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Multiplet quantum numbers and the Gell-Mann--Okubo mass formula}
\label{subsec:gmo}

The $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ decompositions of Eq.~\eqref{eq:su3decomp} organise the light
hadrons into the multiplets observed experimentally: the $q\bar q$ mesons fall into
nonets ($\mathbf{8}\oplus\mathbf{1}$), while the $qqq$ baryons populate an octet and
a decuplet. Each member carries a definite spin-parity fixed by the internal
angular-momentum structure of its constituents.

For a $q\bar q$ meson the total quark spin $S = s_q + s_{\bar q}$ takes the values
$0$ or $1$ (Sect.~\ref{subsec:addition}) and combines with the relative orbital
angular momentum $L$ to give the meson spin $J = L \oplus S$, with $|L-S| \le J \le
L+S$. The state is labelled in spectroscopic notation $n^{\,2S+1}L_J$, where $L = S,
P, D, \dots$ denotes $L = 0, 1, 2, \dots$ and $n$ is the radial quantum number. The
parity and charge-conjugation eigenvalues follow from the spatial wavefunction
$\psi(\vec r) = R(r)\,Y_{lm}(\theta,\phi)$: under parity the spherical harmonic
transforms as $Y_{lm} \to (-1)^L Y_{lm}$, and including the intrinsic odd parity of a
fermion--antifermion pair gives
\begin{equation}
    P(q\bar q) = (-1)^{L+1},
    \qquad
    C(q\bar q) = (-1)^{L+S},
    \label{eq:PC}
\end{equation}
the charge-conjugation eigenvalue being defined only for the neutral, flavourless
members of a multiplet \cite[Ch.~2]{Amsler2018}. The $L = 0$ ground states are
therefore the pseudoscalar mesons ($S=0$, $J^{PC} = 0^{-+}$) and the vector mesons
($S=1$, $J^{PC} = 1^{--}$); for reference the photon carries $J^{PC} = 1^{--}$ and
the $\pi^0$ carries $0^{-+}$. The complete treatment, including the $G$-parity and
the catalogue of allowed and forbidden $J^{PC}$ combinations, is given in the
following chapter on mesons.

Within a multiplet the members would be mass-degenerate if $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ were an
exact symmetry. They are not: the masses increase markedly with hypercharge, by
roughly $130$--$150$~MeV per unit of strangeness, reflecting the larger mass of the
strange quark relative to the up and down quarks. Smaller splittings along the
isospin ($I_3$) axis arise from the electromagnetic interaction and the tiny
$u$--$d$ mass difference, while the overall offsets between distinct multiplets are
due to the spin-dependent (spin--spin and spin--orbit) forces of the strong
interaction \cite[Ch.~13]{Amsler2018}. Treating the symmetry breaking as a
perturbation proportional to the hypercharge, Gell-Mann and Okubo parametrised the
masses of a multiplet as
\begin{equation}
    M = M_0 + M_1\,Y + M_2\!\left[\,I(I+1) - \tfrac{1}{4}Y^2\,\right],
    \label{eq:gmoformula}
\end{equation}
where $M_0$, $M_1$ and $M_2$ are constants fixed for each multiplet. Applied to the
baryon octet, Eq.~\eqref{eq:gmoformula} yields the relation
\begin{equation}
    2\big[\,m(N) + m(\Xi)\,\big] = 3\,m(\Lambda) + m(\Sigma),
\end{equation}
which the measured masses satisfy to better than $1\%$ \cite[Ch.~13]{Amsler2018}.

For the decuplet the isospin and hypercharge of the members are linearly related,
$I = 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}Y$, so the quadratic term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:gmoformula} cancels
and the mass becomes a linear function of $Y$. The masses of the $\Delta$,
$\Sigma^*$, $\Xi^*$ and $\Omega^-$ states, situated at successive hypercharges
$Y = 1, 0, -1, -2$, are therefore equally spaced. This \emph{equal-spacing rule}
predicted the mass of the then-undiscovered $\Omega^-$ from its decuplet partners
$\Sigma^*$ and $\Delta$,
\begin{equation}
    m(\Omega^-) = 3\,m(\Sigma^*) - 2\,m(\Delta) \approx 1691~\mathrm{MeV},
\end{equation}
in striking agreement with the value $m(\Omega^-) = 1672$~MeV measured after its
discovery at Brookhaven in 1964---a landmark confirmation of the $\mathrm{SU}(3)$
classification scheme \cite[Ch.~13]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Colour charge}
\label{subsec:colour}

The most direct evidence for an additional degree of freedom comes from the
spin-$\tfrac{3}{2}$ ground-state baryons composed of three identical quarks, such as
the $\Delta^{++}(uuu)$, the $\Delta^-(ddd)$ and the $\Omega^-(sss)$. For the
$\Omega^-$ the flavour, spin and spatial parts of the wavefunction are each fully
symmetric,
\begin{equation}
    \Omega^- = \ket{sss}\,\ket{\uparrow\uparrow\uparrow}\,\ket{L = 0},
    \label{eq:omega}
\end{equation}
where $L$ is the total orbital angular momentum of the three quarks. Such a state
appears to violate the Pauli principle, which demands that the wavefunction of
identical fermions be antisymmetric under permutations \cite[Ch.~10]{Amsler2018}.

The resolution, due to Greenberg and to Han and Nambu in the mid-1960s, is the
introduction of a new quantum number, \emph{colour}: each quark flavour exists in
three colour states, labelled red ($r$), green ($g$) and blue ($b$), so that a
baryon is a superposition of the six colour permutations rendered antisymmetric by
the colour wavefunction
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    \beta(q_1 q_2 q_3) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}
    \big[\, & q_1(r)q_2(g)q_3(b) + q_1(b)q_2(r)q_3(g) + q_1(g)q_2(b)q_3(r) \\
    & - q_1(g)q_2(r)q_3(b) - q_1(b)q_2(g)q_3(r) - q_1(r)q_2(b)q_3(g) \,\big].
\end{split}
\end{equation}
Multiplying the symmetric flavour-spin-space wavefunction \eqref{eq:omega} by this
antisymmetric colour factor restores the overall antisymmetry and makes the baryon
colour-neutral, or ``white'' \cite[Ch.~10]{Amsler2018}. Antiquarks carry the
corresponding anticolours $\bar{r}, \bar{g}, \bar{b}$, and a meson is a colourless
superposition of a quark and an antiquark of matching anticolour; for the $\pi^+$,
\begin{equation}
    \beta(\pi^+) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}
    \big[\, u(R)\bar{d}(\bar{R}) + u(G)\bar{d}(\bar{G}) + u(B)\bar{d}(\bar{B}) \,\big].
\end{equation}

The interaction between coloured quarks is mediated by gluons, which themselves carry
one colour and one anticolour. Counting the combinations of three colours with three
anticolours gives the same group-theoretical decomposition encountered for the
$q\bar q$ mesons,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{3} \otimes \mathbf{3}^* = \mathbf{8} \oplus \mathbf{1},
\end{equation}
which separates the nine colour--anticolour states into a colour octet and a colour
singlet. The eight octet states are the physical gluons; the singlet combination
$\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\ket{r\bar{r} + g\bar{g} + b\bar{b}}$ is colourless and, being
unconfined, would mediate a long-range strong force that is not observed, so it does
not appear as a physical field \cite[Ch.~10]{Amsler2018}. The exact colour symmetry
$\mathrm{SU}(3)_c$ and its non-Abelian dynamics, including confinement and asymptotic
freedom, are discussed in Sect.~\ref{sec:introduction}.

That colour is a real, threefold degree of freedom is confirmed by several
measurements that count colour states. The decay rate of the $\pi^0 \to 2\gamma$
process proceeds through a quark triangle whose amplitude is enhanced by a factor of
three from the colour sum; the branching fractions of the $W^+$ boson into hadronic
channels carry a factor of three for each available quark colour; and the ratio of
the $e^+e^- \to \mathrm{hadrons}$ cross section to the muon-pair cross section is
multiplied by three relative to the naive quark count. All three observations require
exactly three colours \cite[Ch.~10]{Amsler2018}.
