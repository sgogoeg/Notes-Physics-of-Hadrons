\section{Glueballs and Hybrid Mesons}
\label{sec:glueballs}

Quantum chromodynamics predicts more than the familiar $q\bar q$ mesons and
$qqq$ baryons. Because the gluons that mediate the strong interaction themselves
carry color, the theory admits color-singlet bound states with explicit gluonic
content: \emph{glueballs}, made of glue alone, and \emph{hybrid mesons}, in which
a gluonic excitation is added to a quark--antiquark pair. This chapter reviews the
phenomenological status of these states in the light-quark sector, following
Amsler \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}. We first recall why such states are expected,
establish experimentally that gluons are real dynamical objects, and quote the
mass scale set by lattice QCD. We then assemble the experimental machinery used to
find them: gluon-rich production channels, antiproton--proton annihilation at
rest, the Dalitz plot, the parametrization of resonance line shapes beyond the
simple Breit--Wigner, and partial wave analysis. Because a glueball mixes with
ordinary mesons carrying the same quantum numbers, we must understand the
$q\bar q$ spectrum very well before anything can be claimed about glue; the
chapter therefore works through the scalar nonet puzzle and a modern
coupled-channel view of the scalar spectrum before turning to the tensor sector
and the exotic-$J^{PC}$ hybrid candidates. Compact multiquark states (tetraquarks
and pentaquarks) are deferred to the dedicated chapter on multiquark states.

\subsection{Gluonic matter: glueballs and hybrids}
\label{subsec:gluonic_matter}

The strong interaction is described by the non-Abelian gauge theory
$\mathrm{SU}(3)_c$, whose force carriers are eight massless gluons. Unlike the
photon of quantum electrodynamics, which is electrically neutral, the gluons carry
color charge and therefore couple to one another. This self-coupling is responsible
for the running of the strong coupling constant $\alpha_s$ and for asymptotic
freedom, and it has a striking spectroscopic consequence: the theory permits
color-singlet states built purely from glue. Two-gluon ($gg$) and three-gluon
($ggg$) systems may bind into glueballs, the strong force in its purest form, while
a quark--antiquark pair together with an excited gluonic flux gives a $q\bar q g$
hybrid meson. Both lattice QCD and the flux-tube model predict that glueballs and
hybrids exist \cite[Ch.~10]{Amsler2018,Amsler2018}.

The light-meson sector is the natural proving ground. The cleanest signature of a
state lying outside the quark model is an \emph{exotic} combination of spin, parity
and charge conjugation: the assignment $J^{PC}=1^{-+}$, for example, cannot be
formed by any $q\bar q$ pair (cf.\ Sect.~\ref{sec:groups}). The search proceeds on
two fronts. Among the isoscalar scalar mesons at least two candidates, the
$f_0(1500)$ and the $f_0(1710)$, dispute the status of the ground-state glueball,
while the first excited (tensor) glueball has not yet been identified, even though
more $2^{++}$ states have been reported than the $q\bar q$ nonets can accommodate.
On the second front, two isovector states with exotic quantum numbers $1^{-+}$, the
$\pi_1(1400)$ and the $\pi_1(1600)$, are incompatible with $q\bar q$ assignments and
could be hybrid mesons or multiquark states \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Seeing gluons: multi-jet events}
\label{subsec:multijet}

Before hunting for bound states of glue it is worth recalling that the gluon is an
experimentally established quantum. In electron--positron annihilation,
$e^+e^-\to\gamma^*\to q\bar q$, the quark and antiquark fragment into two
back-to-back jets of hadrons. Hard-gluon bremsstrahlung off one of the quarks,
$q\bar q g$, produces a third jet; the three-jet topology observed at the PETRA
collider in 1979 was the first direct evidence for the gluon. Higher-order
processes, in which the radiated gluon itself splits ($g\to gg$) or a secondary
$q\bar q$ pair is produced, give rise to four-jet events.

The relative rates of two-, three- and four-jet events and their angular
correlations cannot be described without the gluon and its self-interaction. Their
quantitative analysis at LEP, for instance by the ALEPH experiment, both measures
$\alpha_s$ and confirms that the gluon is a spin-$1$ boson belonging to a
non-Abelian gauge theory. The reality of gluons as dynamical, self-interacting
objects is the physical prerequisite for expecting them to form bound states.

\subsection{The glueball spectrum from lattice QCD}
\label{subsec:lattice}

Lattice gauge theory predicts a rich spectrum of glueballs. In the quenched
approximation the ground state is a scalar, $0^{++}$, with a mass of about
$1700~\mathrm{MeV}$ (with an uncertainty of order $100~\mathrm{MeV}$); the first
excited state is a tensor, $2^{++}$, near $2400~\mathrm{MeV}$, and the lightest
glueball carrying exotic quantum numbers, $2^{+-}$, lies around
$4200~\mathrm{MeV}$ \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

This prediction comes with an important complication. The low-mass glueballs fall
in the same mass region as ordinary isoscalar $q\bar q$ states --- the scalar near
the $1^3P_0$ ($0^{++}$) mesons and the tensor near the $2^3P_2$, $3^3P_2$ and
$1^3F_2$ ($2^{++}$) mesons --- so a glueball will mix with nearby $q\bar q$ states
of the same quantum numbers. The lattice values quoted above are obtained in the
quenched approximation, which neglects virtual $q\bar q$ pairs and hence this
mixing, although first calculations including quark loops suggest that the mass
shifts are small. The practical consequence is that the scalar sector contains more
isoscalar states than a single $0^{++}$ $q\bar q$ nonet can hold: the lightest
scalars, those below $1~\mathrm{GeV}$, are most naturally interpreted as four-quark
states, while the ground-state $1^3P_0$ $q\bar q$ nonet lies in the
$1400~\mathrm{MeV}$ region. Identifying the scalar glueball therefore requires a
good understanding of \emph{all} the scalar states \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Strategies for the glueball search}
\label{subsec:strategies}

Glueball production is expected to be enhanced in gluon-rich environments and
suppressed where the dynamics is governed by photons, which couple to the electric
charges of quarks rather than to gluons. Two-photon collisions therefore act as an
``anti-glueball filter'': a glueball should be suppressed in $\gamma\gamma$
production, a feature exploited at $e^+e^-$ machines (JADE, L3, ALEPH and Belle).
We shall apply this filter quantitatively to the scalar candidates in
Sect.~\ref{subsec:scalar_glueball}.

Conversely, three classes of reaction are gluon-rich and so favorable for glueball
formation. The first is central production in high-energy hadron collisions, in
which the incident projectiles are scattered through small angles and a gluon-rich
system is produced centrally by double-Pomeron exchange; this channel was studied
by the WA91 and WA102 experiments at CERN and more recently at LHCb. The second is
the radiative decay $J/\psi(1S)\to\gamma\,+\,\text{light hadrons}$, in which the
$c\bar c$ pair annihilates through intermediate gluons (MARK~III, DM2 and BES at
Beijing). The third is antiproton--proton annihilation, both at rest and in flight,
studied by ASTERIX, Crystal Barrel and OBELIX at LEAR. The future PANDA experiment
at FAIR will continue this program with antiproton beams
\cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Antiproton--proton annihilation at rest}
\label{subsec:annihilation}

Annihilation at rest is valuable because it prepares the reaction in a small number
of well-defined initial states. A low-momentum antiproton entering a liquid
hydrogen ($\mathrm{LH_2}$) or deuterium ($\mathrm{LD_2}$) target is captured by a
proton to form protonium, a $\bar p p$ atom, at a large principal quantum number
$n\approx 30$. The atom then cascades toward low $n$ through radiative (X-ray) and
Auger transitions. In a liquid target the dominant effect is \emph{Stark mixing}:
collisions with neighboring atoms mix the otherwise degenerate $\ell$-states, so
that annihilation proceeds predominantly from atomic $S$-states even at higher $n$.
In a gaseous target circular cascades become important, and in vacuum they dominate
because the Stark effect is absent. The choice of target density therefore tunes
the fractions of $S$-wave and $P$-wave annihilation \cite[Ch.~3]{Amsler2018}.

Annihilation from a given atomic state fixes the initial quantum numbers. For a
fermion--antifermion system the parity, charge conjugation and $G$ parity are
\begin{equation}
  P = -(-1)^{L}, \qquad
  C = (-1)^{L+S}, \qquad
  G = (-1)^{L+S+I}.
  \label{eq:ppbar_PCG}
\end{equation}
Annihilation from the two $S$-wave states therefore gives
$\,^1S_0\;(L=0,\,S=0)\to J^{PC}=0^{-+}$, with $I^{G}(J^{PC})=1^{-}(0^{-+})$ or
$0^{+}(0^{-+})$, and $\,^3S_1\;(L=0,\,S=1)\to 1^{--}$, with $I^{G}=1^{+}$ or
$0^{-}$. From the $P$-wave atom one obtains $\,^1P_1\to 1^{+-}$, $\,^3P_0\to 0^{++}$,
$\,^3P_1\to 1^{++}$ and $\,^3P_2\to 2^{++}$.

Two features make this an exceptionally clean laboratory for spectroscopy. First,
conservation of the quantum numbers in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ppbar_PCG} severely restricts
which initial states can feed a given final state; for the $3\pi$ system, for
instance, $G_{3\pi}=-1$ and $C_{3\pi^0}=+1$ select only a handful of $\,^{2S+1}L_J$
initial states. Second, different initial atomic states do not interfere with one
another. Together these properties greatly reduce the number of amplitudes that
must be considered in a partial wave analysis \cite[Ch.~3]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Multiparticle final states and the Dalitz plot}
\label{subsec:dalitz}

In a three-body final state $M_1 M_2 M_3$ a resonance reveals itself as an
enhancement in the invariant mass of a pair of particles,
\begin{equation}
  M^2 = \left(E_i + E_j\right)^2 - \left(\vec p_i + \vec p_j\right)^2 .
  \label{eq:invariant_mass}
\end{equation}
A single one-dimensional projection, however, superimposes the dynamics of the
other two pairs, so one variable does not capture the full reaction. The number of
independent variables follows from simple counting. Three four-vectors carry twelve
quantities; energy--momentum conservation removes four, the three known particle
masses remove three more, and the absence of a preferred orientation in space
(three Euler angles) removes a final three, leaving exactly two. Choosing the two
invariant-mass-squared combinations $m_{12}^2$ and $m_{23}^2$ defines the Dalitz
plot, which contains the complete dynamics of the decay \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

The Dalitz plot has three properties that make it the central tool of light-meson
spectroscopy. Pure phase space populates it uniformly, so a structureless reaction
gives a flat distribution. A resonance in one of the two-body subsystems produces a
band at the corresponding resonance mass. And the kinematic boundary depends on the
parent mass, so the available phase space --- in particular the lower-left edge of
the plot --- differs for $\eta$, for $\bar p p$, and for a heavier parent decaying
to the same three particles. Figure~\ref{fig:dalitz_boundary} shows the boundary for
$(\bar p p)_{\text{rest}}\to\pi^+\pi^-\pi^0$ together with the three $\rho(770)$
bands.

The spin of a resonance is encoded in the geometry of its decay. In the resonance
rest frame the two daughters emerge back-to-back, and the helicity angle $\theta$
between a daughter momentum and the spectator (or beam) direction reflects the
resonance spin and modulates the intensity along the band, as sketched in
Fig.~\ref{fig:dalitz_geometry}.

The classic illustration is $(\bar p p)_{\text{rest}}\to\pi^+\pi^-\pi^0$, whose
Dalitz plot shows clear bands from the $\rho^+(770)$, $\rho^-(770)$ and
$\rho^0(770)$, together with a contribution from the $f_2(1270)$, and strong
interference effects at the crossings of the $\rho$ bands. The three $\rho$ charge
states appear with approximately equal strength, which requires the annihilating
$\bar p p$ to be in $I=0$, so that the $\,^3S_1$ initial state dominates. In the
$\rho\pi$ basis the $(I,I_3)=(0,0)$ and $(1,0)$ combinations are
\begin{align}
  \ket{0,0} &= \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}
      \bigl(\ket{\rho^+\pi^-} - \ket{\rho^0\pi^0} + \ket{\rho^-\pi^+}\bigr),
      \label{eq:rhopi_I0}\\[2pt]
  \ket{1,0} &= \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
      \bigl(\ket{\rho^+\pi^-} - \ket{\rho^-\pi^+}\bigr).
      \label{eq:rhopi_I1}
\end{align}
A first contributing amplitude is therefore $\,^3S_1\to\rho\pi$ followed by
$\rho\to\pi\pi$, that is $0^{-}(1^{--})\to 1^{+}(1^{-})\,1^{-}(0^{-})$
\cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Resonance line shapes: poles and the Flatt\'e parametrization}
\label{subsec:lineshapes}

Extracting resonances from a Dalitz plot requires a dynamical amplitude for each
intermediate state, and here the non-relativistic Breit--Wigner of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:breit-wigner-amplitude} is often not good enough. A resonance is
properly an eigenstate of the scattering operator $S$, whose matrix elements
$S_{fi}=\bra{f}S\ket{i}$ connect initial and final states. Probability
conservation makes $S$ unitary, $S^\dagger S = S S^\dagger = 1$, and the
scattering amplitude $T$ is introduced through
\begin{equation}
  S = 1 + 2i\,T .
  \label{eq:smatrix}
\end{equation}
For a single channel the Breit--Wigner amplitude
$T = e^{i\delta}\sin\delta$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:amplitude-phaseshift} satisfies
unitarity automatically. As soon as a resonance can decay into several final
states, however --- the $a_0(980)$, for example, decays into both $\eta\pi$ and
$K\bar K$ --- the operator $S$ must genuinely be treated as a matrix in channel
space, and unitarity couples the channels to one another
\cite[Ch.~12]{Amsler2018}.

At the same time light-meson resonances are broad, so the amplitude must be
relativistic. The propagator of a stable particle of mass $m_0$ behaves as
$T(s)\sim 1/(m_0^2-s)$; for an unstable particle the replacement
$m_0\to m_0 - i\Gamma/2$ gives, for $\Gamma\ll m_0$,
\begin{equation}
  T(s) \sim \frac{1}{m_0^2 - s - i\,m_0\Gamma},
  \label{eq:rel_bw}
\end{equation}
the relativistic Breit--Wigner amplitude. In the multichannel case the relativistic
amplitude $\hat T$ is related to the unitary $S$-matrix by
$S = 1 + 2i\sqrt{\rho}\,\hat T\sqrt{\rho}$, where the diagonal matrix
$\rho$ contains the two-body phase-space factors $\rho_i = 2q_i/m$, with $q_i$ the
breakup momentum in channel $i$ \cite[Ch.~12]{Amsler2018}.

For a single resonance coupled to two channels these requirements lead to the
\emph{Flatt\'e parametrization},
\begin{equation}
  \hat T =
  \frac{m_0\Gamma_0}
       {m_0^2 - m^2 - i\,m_0\Gamma_0\!\left(\rho_1\gamma_1^2 + \rho_2\gamma_2^2\right)}
  \begin{pmatrix} \gamma_1^2 & \gamma_1\gamma_2 \\[2pt]
                  \gamma_2\gamma_1 & \gamma_2^2 \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad \gamma_1^2 + \gamma_2^2 = 1,
  \label{eq:flatte}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_i$ is the coupling of the resonance to channel $i$ and the
mass-dependent total width obeys
$\Gamma_0(m_0) = \sum_i\tilde\Gamma_i = \Gamma_0\sum_i\gamma_i^2\rho_i(m_0)$. The
width in the denominator is shared among the channels: the resonance has a single
lifetime, whichever final state is observed, regardless of whether a given channel
accounts for $20\%$ or $60\%$ of the decays \cite[Ch.~12]{Amsler2018}.

The physical resonance parameters are not the input constants $m_0$ and $\Gamma_0$
but the position of the \emph{pole} of $\hat T$ in the complex energy plane, found
by scanning the complex $s$ plane for the zero of the denominator in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:flatte}. For an isolated narrow resonance the pole sits at
$m_P = m_0 - i\Gamma/2$ and the distinction is immaterial, but for strongly coupled
channels the line shape observed on the real axis can differ drastically from a
Breit--Wigner of width $\Gamma_0$. The $a_0(980)$ illustrates the effect: it sits
almost exactly on the $K\bar K$ threshold ($m_0 = 998.2~\mathrm{MeV}$,
$\Gamma_0 = 301.4~\mathrm{MeV}$). Below threshold the phase-space factor $\rho_2$
is imaginary, so the $K\bar K$ term in the denominator becomes real and shifts the
peak rather than broadening it; for comparable couplings
($\gamma_2^2/\gamma_1^2\approx 1$) one decay channel ``eats into'' the other and
the visible $\eta\pi$ peak narrows from roughly $300$ to about $50~\mathrm{MeV}$,
as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:flatte_a0980}. The resonance \emph{looks} very narrow,
but the underlying situation is far more complicated, and for such at-threshold
states the location of the pole on the different Riemann sheets of the
multivalued amplitude becomes essential for a meaningful determination of the
resonance parameters \cite[Ch.~12]{Amsler2018}.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{Figures/flatte_a0980.pdf}
  \caption{Flatt\'e line shape of the $a_0(980)$ in the $\eta\pi^0$ channel,
           Eq.~\eqref{eq:flatte}, with $m_0=998.2~\mathrm{MeV}$ and
           $\Gamma_0=301.4~\mathrm{MeV}$. With the $K\bar K$ channel switched off

\end{figure}

The same threshold physics governs the celebrated $\chi_{c1}(3872)$ in the
charmonium region, which lies within a fraction of an $\mathrm{MeV}$ of the
$D^0\bar D^{*0}$ threshold. As that channel opens, unitarity forces a sudden
breakdown of the intensity in the $J/\psi\,\pi\pi$ channel, and the line shapes
observed in $J/\psi\,\pi\pi$ and in $D^0\bar D^{*0}$ differ markedly from one
another. The lesson is general: a resonance is characterized by its complex pole
position, while peak positions and apparent widths are channel-dependent
artifacts of the analytic structure near thresholds \cite[Ch.~12]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Angular distributions and partial wave analysis}
\label{subsec:pwa}

The observed intensity over the Dalitz plot is the modulus squared of a coherent
sum of amplitudes. For a given set of overall quantum numbers it may be written
\begin{equation}
  I_{J^{PC}}(s_1,s_2) =
      \int_{\Delta s_{12}\,\Delta s_{13}}
      \left| \sum_{\text{perm}} \sum_{L,\ell}
             A_{J^{PC},L,\ell} \right|^{2},
  \label{eq:intensity}
\end{equation}
with each partial amplitude factorizing into three pieces,
\begin{equation}
  A_{J^{PC},L,\ell}
     = Z_{J^{PC},L,\ell}(\vec p,\vec q)\,
       \cdot D_L(\vec p)\,
       \cdot F_\ell(\vec p,\vec q),
  \label{eq:amplitude}
\end{equation}
where $Z$ carries the angular dependence (and hence the spin information), $D_L$ is
the centrifugal-barrier factor, and $F_\ell$ is the dynamical amplitude of
Sect.~\ref{subsec:lineshapes} --- a relativistic Breit--Wigner, a Flatt\'e
amplitude, or a more general coupled-channel parametrization
\cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018,Amsler2018}. Here $L$ denotes the orbital angular momentum
of the resonance relative to the spectator and $\ell$ the relative angular momentum
of the two mesons into which the resonance decays.

The angular factor turns the helicity-angle distribution into a sensitive spin
analyzer. For the reaction $\bar p p(\,^1S_0)\to X\eta\to\pi\pi\,\eta$ the
distribution in $\cos\theta$ is essentially $\lvert P_J(\cos\theta)\rvert^2$: it is
flat for a scalar resonance ($0^{+}\to 0^{-}0^{-}$, $\ell=0$), proportional to
$\cos^2\theta$ for a vector ($1^{-}\to 0^{-}0^{-}$, $\ell=1$), and a structured,
doubly peaked distribution proportional to $(3\cos^2\theta-1)^2/4$ for a tensor
($2^{+}\to 0^{-}0^{-}$, $\ell=2$). These markedly different shapes,
Fig.~\ref{fig:angular_distributions}, allow a partial wave analysis (PWA) to
determine the $J^{PC}$ of the contributing resonances. One caveat must be kept in
mind: such single-resonance distributions show $I=|A_{J^{PC},L,\ell}|^2$ for one
amplitude in isolation, whereas in a real PWA all amplitudes fed by one $\bar p p$
initial state are summed coherently and interfere. States are then named
according to $I^{G}(J^{PC}):X_J(\text{mass})$, with isospin-$1$ states written
$a_J(\text{mass})$ and isospin-$0$ states $f_J(\text{mass})$.

  \label{fig:angular_distributions}

A word of caution is essential: not every peak in a one-dimensional projection is a
resonance. Reflections of genuine bands and interference between amplitudes can
produce structures in a mass spectrum that correspond to no physical state. Only a
full amplitude analysis over the Dalitz plot, incorporating the correct angular
structure, reliably separates resonances from kinematic artifacts. The projection of
the $(\bar p p)_{\text{rest}}\to\pi^0\pi^0\eta$ Dalitz plot, in which a prominent
bump in the $M^2(\pi^0\eta)$ spectrum is partly a reflection rather than a state, is
the textbook example \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

The converse is equally powerful: a PWA can reveal that a resonance is
\emph{missing} from the model. In practice the parameters of the amplitudes are
determined by minimizing
\begin{equation}
  \chi^2 = \sum_{i\,\in\,\text{cells}}
           \frac{\left(N_i^{\text{exp}}\cdot c_i - N_i^{\text{theo}}\right)^2}
                {\sigma_i^2},
  \label{eq:pwa_chi2}
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over the cells of the Dalitz plot, $N_i^{\text{exp}}$ and
$N_i^{\text{theo}}$ are the measured and modeled populations, and the factors
$c_i$ correct for the detector acceptance. A fit of poor quality whose residuals
cluster \emph{systematically} in particular regions of the plot --- rather than
scattering randomly --- signals that an amplitude has been left out.

The benchmark case is the Crystal Barrel measurement of
$(\bar p n)_{\text{rest}}\to\eta\pi^-\pi^0$, obtained with antiprotons stopping in
liquid deuterium and a slow spectator proton; the $\bar p n$ system is a pure
isospin-$1$ initial state, annihilating from $\,^3S_1$ and $\,^1P_1$. The Dalitz
plot is dominated by $\bar p n\to\rho^-(770)\eta$ (about $58\%$ of the intensity)
and $\bar p n\to a_2(1320)\pi$ (about $15\%$), with the contributions of
$\rho^-(1450)\eta$, $a_2(1650)\pi$, $a_0(980)\pi$ and $a_0(1450)\pi$ all found to
be negligible. A fit restricted to these conventional amplitudes fails, with
$\chi^2/\mathrm{d.o.f.} = 3.07$ and coherent islands of deviation between fit and
data across the plot. Adding a single further amplitude with \emph{exotic}
quantum numbers $J^{PC}=1^{-+}$ in the $\eta\pi$ system --- allowed from both
$\,^3S_1$ and $\,^1P_1$ --- resolves the discrepancy: the final fit yields
$\chi^2/\mathrm{d.o.f.} = \chi^2/(411-20) = 1.29$ for $411$ Dalitz-plot cells and
$20$ free parameters. The exotic wave interferes with the $\ell=2$ amplitude of
the $a_2(1320)$, and the corresponding state is the $\pi_1(1400)$, to which we
return in Sect.~\ref{subsec:hybrids} \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Sorting the scalar nonet}
\label{subsec:scalar_nonet}

Glueballs mix with mesons carrying the same quantum numbers, so before any state
can be declared supernumerary we must know which scalars belong to the ordinary
$q\bar q$ nonet. The quantum numbers $I^{G}(J^{PC})=0^{+}(0^{++})$ correspond to a
quark--antiquark pair with parallel spins and one unit of orbital excitation,
$S=1$ and $L=1$. The same configuration produces the triplet
$0^{++}$, $1^{++}$, $2^{++}$, that is, the isoscalars $f_0$, $f_1$, $f_2$ and the
isovectors $a_0$, $a_1$, $a_2$. The masses of the established partners,
$a_1(1260)$, $a_2(1320)$, $f_1(1285)$ and $f_2(1270)$, lie close together, showing
that the spin-orbit forces are not very large; the scalar members $a_0$ and $f_0$
of the $1^3P_0$ nonet are therefore expected at a similar mass, around
$1300~\mathrm{MeV}$ \cite[Ch.~5]{Amsler2018}.

The nonet requires \emph{two} isoscalar $f_0$ states, mixtures of the SU(3) octet
and singlet configurations as introduced in Sect.~\ref{sec:mesons}. Their mass
splitting may amount to a few hundred $\mathrm{MeV}$ --- compare the
$f_2(1270)$--$f_2'(1525)$ splitting of about $250~\mathrm{MeV}$ and the
$\eta$--$\eta'$ splitting of about $400~\mathrm{MeV}$ --- and the mixing angle can
of course differ from nonet to nonet. Whatever the angle, one of the two
isoscalars carries the $s\bar s$ component and must therefore show a substantial
$K\bar K$ decay mode \cite[Ch.~5]{Amsler2018}.

The Particle Data Group lists as candidates the isovectors $a_0(980)$
(width $50$--$100~\mathrm{MeV}$) and $a_0(1450)$ ($258\pm14~\mathrm{MeV}$), and
the isoscalars $f_0(500)$ (very broad), $f_0(980)$ ($10$--$100~\mathrm{MeV}$),
$f_0(1370)$ ($200$--$500~\mathrm{MeV}$), $f_0(1500)$ (about $110~\mathrm{MeV}$)
and $f_0(1710)$ (about $150~\mathrm{MeV}$); the last two are conspicuously narrow
compared to the others. The masses of the heavier states have the right order of
magnitude for the $1^3P_0$ nonet, but the assignment immediately runs into
trouble: the $f_0(1370)$ and $f_0(1500)$ are very close in mass, and \emph{both}
have weak $K\bar K$ decay modes, so they cannot plausibly be the
$n\bar n$--$s\bar s$ pair of the same nonet \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

The resolution requires stepping outside the pure $q\bar q$ picture twice. First,
the light scalars $f_0(500)$ (also known as $\sigma$), $K_0^*(700)$ (the
$\kappa$), $f_0(980)$ and $a_0(980)$ form a separate low-mass nonet of four-quark
or meson--meson (molecular) states; the strong $K\bar K$ affinity of the
$a_0(980)$ and $f_0(980)$, impossible for an isovector $q\bar q$ state, supports
this interpretation, to which we return in the multiquark chapter. The
ground-state $1^3P_0$ $q\bar q$ nonet is then plausibly built from the
$a_0(1450)$, the $K_0^*(1430)$, the $f_0(1370)$ as the mostly $n\bar n$
isoscalar, and the $f_0(1710)$ as the mostly $s\bar s$ isoscalar. Second, this
assignment leaves the $f_0(1500)$ without a home: it is too light to be a radial
excitation, and it cannot be the missing $s\bar s$ state, whose role is taken by
the $f_0(1710)$. Having so far considered only $q\bar q$ states, we are led to
ask whether the supernumerary $f_0(1500)$ is the scalar glueball
\cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{The scalar glueball and mixing with $q\bar q$ states}
\label{subsec:scalar_glueball}

The decisive data on the scalar sector came from stopped-antiproton annihilation at
LEAR, studied with the Crystal Barrel detector. Dalitz plots of all-neutral final
states reveal a rich scalar and tensor content. The reaction
$(\bar p p)_{\text{rest}}\to\pi^0\pi^0\pi^0$ shows the $0^{++}$ states $f_0(500)$
(the broad $\sigma$), $f_0(980)$, $f_0(1370)$ and $f_0(1500)$, together with the
$2^{++}$ states $f_2(1270)$ and $f_2(1565)$. The reaction
$(\bar p p)_{\text{rest}}\to\pi^0\pi^0\eta$ shows the $1^{-}(0^{++})$ states
$a_0(980)$ and $a_0(1450)$ and the $1^{-}(2^{++})$ state $a_2(1320)$, together with
the $f_0(980)$. The reaction $(\bar p p)_{\text{rest}}\to\pi^0\eta\eta$ shows the
$a_0(980)$ and the scalars $f_0(1370)$ and $f_0(1500)$ \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

To test whether the supernumerary $f_0(1500)$ of
Sect.~\ref{subsec:scalar_nonet} is the glueball, one uses its
$\mathrm{SU}(3)_f$ decay couplings. Recall the ideal nonet mixing introduced in
Sect.~\ref{sec:mesons}: the mostly-strange isoscalar is
\begin{equation}
  \ket{f'} = \sqrt{\tfrac{2}{3}}\,\ket{f_8} - \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{3}}\,\ket{f_1}
           = -\ket{s\bar s},
  \label{eq:fprime_ideal}
\end{equation}
and the mostly-nonstrange isoscalar is
\begin{equation}
  \ket{f} = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{3}}\,\ket{f_8} + \sqrt{\tfrac{2}{3}}\,\ket{f_1}
          = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\,\ket{u\bar u + d\bar d} \equiv \ket{n\bar n},
  \label{eq:f_ideal}
\end{equation}
with the general physical state written $\ket{f_\alpha}=\cos\alpha\,\ket{n\bar n}
-\sin\alpha\,\ket{s\bar s}$. A pure glueball decays \emph{flavor blind}: it
couples equally to all flavors and is therefore a flavor singlet,
\begin{equation}
  \ket{G} = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\,\ket{u\bar u + d\bar d + s\bar s},
  \label{eq:glueball_singlet}
\end{equation}
which gives, using the SU(3) coupling coefficients and apart from phase-space
factors, the characteristic pattern of decay rates into pairs of pseudoscalars
\begin{equation}
  G \to \pi\pi : \eta\eta : \eta\eta' : K\bar K = 3 : 1 : 0 : 4 .
  \label{eq:glueball_ratios}
\end{equation}
The coupling to $\eta\eta'$ vanishes for an ideal flavor-singlet glueball
\cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

The Crystal Barrel measurements confront this prediction directly. For the
$f_0(1500)$ the measured rates are
\begin{equation}
  f_0(1500) \to \pi\pi : \eta\eta : \eta\eta' : K\bar K
  \;=\; 3 : \,\sim\!0.4 : \,\sim\!1.5 : \,\sim\!0.7 ,
  \label{eq:f1500_ratios}
\end{equation}
in striking disagreement with Eq.~\eqref{eq:glueball_ratios}: the $K\bar K$ rate
is far too small and, above all, the $\eta\eta'$ mode --- strictly forbidden for a
flavor singlet --- is large. (The current world averages,
$3:\sim\!0.5:\sim\!0.2:\sim\!0.7$, soften the $\eta\eta'$ anomaly but confirm the
suppressed $K\bar K$ rate.) The $f_0(1500)$ does \emph{not} decay flavor blind
and is therefore not a \emph{pure} glueball \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

The natural interpretation is glueball--meson mixing. Since the lattice places
the bare glueball $G_0$ in the very mass region of the $1^3P_0$ isoscalars, the
physical $f_0(1370)$, $f_0(1500)$ and $f_0(1710)$ are superpositions of the
$n\bar n$, $s\bar s$ and glue basis states, and the interference between the
components naturally accounts for the observed pattern of $\pi\pi$, $\eta\eta$,
$\eta\eta'$ and $K\bar K$ rates. The decomposition, however, is model dependent.
In the scheme of Sexton, Vaccarino and Weingarten the bare glueball lies
\emph{above} the $s\bar s$ state and the glue concentrates in the heaviest
state, $f_0(1710) \approx 0.34\,\ket{n\bar n} + 0.33\,\ket{s\bar s} +
0.88\,\ket{G}$, whereas in the scheme of Close and Kirk the bare glueball lies
\emph{between} $n\bar n$ and $s\bar s$ and the glue concentrates in the middle
state, $f_0(1500) \approx -0.62\,\ket{n\bar n} + 0.37\,\ket{s\bar s} -
0.69\,\ket{G}$, with the $f_0(1370)$ and $f_0(1710)$ remaining dominantly
$n\bar n$ and $s\bar s$ with small glue admixtures. Further schemes exist. In the
Crystal Barrel/WA102 scenario the $f_0(1500)$ is mostly glue with only about
$20\%$ $q\bar q$ admixture
% TODO: add \cite{Sexton1995} and \cite{CloseKirk2001} once in the .bib
\cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

Several production arguments support assigning the dominant $s\bar s$ character
to the $f_0(1710)$ rather than to the $f_0(1500)$. The $f_0(1710)$ decays
preferentially into $K\bar K$ and is barely seen in $\bar p p$ annihilation,
as expected from the OZI rule since the proton contains no constituent $s$
quark. Because there is insufficient phase space to produce it in annihilation at
rest, Crystal Barrel searched for it with higher-momentum antiprotons in
$\bar p p\to\pi^0\eta\eta$ at $900~\mathrm{MeV}/c$: the $f_0(1500)$ is clearly
seen while the $f_0(1710)\to\eta\eta$ is not. The two-photon ``anti-glueball
filter'' of Sect.~\ref{subsec:strategies} points the same way, though less
decisively than one might hope: Belle clearly observes the $f_0(980)$ in
$\gamma\gamma\to\pi\pi$, and the $S$-wave of $\gamma\gamma\to K_sK_s$ favors the
presence of the $f_0(1710)$ while the $f_0(1500)$ is not seen --- consistent with
a glue-dominated $f_0(1500)$ suppressed in photon collisions --- but the
extracted $D$-wave looks very similar to the $S$-wave, so the experimental
situation remains unclear and further data are needed. The classification
therefore stays controversial: alternative schemes promote the $f_0(1710)$ to
ground-state glueball, and lattice calculations including dynamical quarks may
ultimately settle the question \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{A coupled-channel view of the scalar spectrum}
\label{subsec:coupled_channel}

The discussion so far rested on analyses of individual reactions. Since the
scalars are broad, overlapping and coupled to many final states, the modern
approach is a simultaneous coupled-channel partial wave analysis of all available
data, with amplitudes of the type discussed in Sect.~\ref{subsec:lineshapes}
respecting unitarity across channels. The most comprehensive such analysis to
date, by the Bonn--Gatchina group
% TODO: replace by \cite{Sarantsev2021} once in the .bib
(Phys.\ Lett.\ B 816 (2021) 136227), fits simultaneously the BESIII radiative
decays $J/\psi\to\gamma\pi^0\pi^0$, $\gamma K_sK_s$, $\gamma\eta\eta'$ and
$\gamma\omega\phi$; the Crystal Barrel annihilation channels in liquid and
gaseous hydrogen ($3\pi^0$, $\pi^0\pi^+\pi^-$, $2\pi^0\eta$, $\pi^0\eta\eta$, and
the $K\bar K\pi$ modes) and in liquid deuterium ($\pi^+\pi^-\pi^-$,
$\pi^0\pi^0\pi^-$, $K_sK^-\pi^0$, $K_sK_s\pi^-$); and the $\pi\pi$ scattering
data into $\pi\pi$, $\eta\eta$, $\eta\eta'$ and $K^+K^-$ from CERN--Munich, GAMS
and BNL. The combined fit describes all data sets with $\chi^2/N$ close to unity.

The outcome is a coherent picture of \emph{ten} isoscalar scalar resonances up to
about $2.3~\mathrm{GeV}$, which fall, according to their interference patterns
and decay couplings, into two families. One family has wavefunctions with large
SU(3) \emph{singlet} components: the $f_0(500)$, $f_0(1370)$, $f_0(1710)$,
$f_0(2020)$ and $f_0(2200)$. The other has large \emph{octet} components: the
$f_0(980)$, $f_0(1500)$, $f_0(1770)$, $f_0(2100)$ and $f_0(2330)$. Notably, the
analysis resolves the structure near $1.7~\mathrm{GeV}$ into two distinct states,
the singlet-like $f_0(1710)$ at $1700\pm18~\mathrm{MeV}$ and an octet-like
$f_0(1770)$ at $1765\pm15~\mathrm{MeV}$, which earlier single-channel analyses
could not separate.

Radiative $J/\psi$ decay now acts as a flavor filter. The $c\bar c$ pair
annihilates into a photon and two gluons, and the two-gluon system, like the
glueball state $\ket{G}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:glueball_singlet}, is an SU(3) singlet.
Direct production therefore feeds only the singlet components of the scalar
wavefunctions; octet-dominated states can be produced solely through whatever
glue admixture $\ket{G}$ they contain. The measured $J/\psi\to\gamma f_0$ decay
rates, plotted as a function of the resonance mass, do not single out one
resonance but rise to a broad peak in the region around
$1900~\mathrm{MeV}$ before falling off. The interpretation offered by the
analysis is that this peak traces the scalar glueball itself: rather than
appearing as one additional narrow state, the glueball is mixed into, and
distributed over, the scalar mesons in this mass range, enhancing their
production in the gluon-rich radiative decay.

This conclusion comes with explicit caveats. Above $1700~\mathrm{MeV}$ the fit is
constrained almost exclusively by the $J/\psi\to\gamma X$ data; the substantial
$4\pi$ decay modes of the scalars are not yet included in the coupled-channel
analysis; a more detailed theoretical understanding of the mixing is needed; and
no supernumerary state is directly observed --- the glueball is inferred from
production systematics, not counted as an extra resonance. Mapping the glueball
spectrum, including the tensor ground state expected near $2400~\mathrm{MeV}$,
will require gluon-rich data of high statistics: central production, whose yield
falls only like $1/M_R^2$ with the produced mass; radiative $J/\psi$ decays,
which reach up to about $3~\mathrm{GeV}$ and whose statistics have recently
improved dramatically; and antiproton--proton annihilation in flight at PANDA,
which extends well beyond the mass range accessible at LEAR.

\subsection{Tensor mesons and the excited glueball}
\label{subsec:tensor}

Because the first excited glueball is predicted to be a tensor near
$2400~\mathrm{MeV}$, the isoscalar tensor sector deserves attention. The ideally
mixed $1^3P_2$ $q\bar q$ states, the $f_2(1270)$ (largely $n\bar n$) and the
$f_2'(1525)$ (largely $s\bar s$), are well established. Between about
$1525~\mathrm{MeV}$ and $2500~\mathrm{MeV}$, however, eight isoscalar tensor mesons
have been reported, whereas only six are expected to belong to the $2^3P_2$,
$3^3P_2$ and $1^3F_2$ nonets. This excess hints at the presence of a tensor
glueball \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

The difficulty is that these tensor states are broad and overlapping, so they
interfere strongly and none can yet be assigned definitively as the glueball; the
sector awaits the kind of coupled-channel treatment now applied to the scalars.
The OZI-suppressed reaction $\bar p p\to\phi\phi$, which can nevertheless proceed
through the emission of glueballs, is one promising channel for isolating the
tensor glueball. It is worth noting in passing that some of these states may have
a different origin altogether: the $f_2(1565)$, for example, could be a
nucleon--antinucleon bound state shaken out of protonium. Such baryonia have
remained experimentally elusive, perhaps because the onset of annihilation at short
distances broadens them substantially \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

\subsection{Hybrid mesons and $J^{PC}=1^{-+}$ exotics}
\label{subsec:hybrids}

A hybrid meson is a quark--antiquark pair bound together with an excited gluonic
flux tube. It may be an isoscalar or an isovector and, crucially, it can carry
exotic values of $J^{PC}$. The flux-tube model and lattice QCD both place the
lightest hybrids in the range $1.7$--$1.9~\mathrm{GeV}$, with quantum numbers
$0^{-+}$, $1^{-+}$, $1^{--}$ and $2^{-+}$; most are expected to be broad, though
some may be as narrow as $100~\mathrm{MeV}$. The unmistakable experimental signature
is the assignment $J^{PC}=1^{-+}$, which no $q\bar q$ pair can have (cf.\
Sect.~\ref{sec:groups}) \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

Two isovector $1^{-+}$ candidates are known. The $\pi_1(1400)$, about
$350~\mathrm{MeV}$ broad, decays into $\eta\pi$; its $\ell=1$ amplitude interferes
with the $\ell=2$ amplitude of the $a_2(1320)\to\eta\pi$, producing a
forward--backward asymmetry in the $\eta\pi$ angular distribution. It was reported
at BNL in $\pi^-p\to\eta\pi^0 n$ with $18~\mathrm{GeV}$ pions and established by
Crystal Barrel in $\bar p n\to\pi^-\pi^0\eta$, where, as shown in
Sect.~\ref{subsec:pwa}, the partial wave analysis fails without the exotic wave
($\chi^2/\mathrm{d.o.f.}=3.07$) and succeeds once the $\pi_1(1400)$ is included
($\chi^2/\mathrm{d.o.f.}=1.29$). The $\pi_1(1600)$ is a second $1^{-+}$ state,
reported in diffractive $\pi^- N$ interactions; it decays into $\rho\pi$,
$b_1(1235)\pi$ and $f_1(1285)\pi$ but not into $\eta\pi$. A clear $\rho^0\pi^-$
signal, for instance, is seen by the COMPASS experiment in
$\pi^- \mathrm{Pb}\to\pi^+\pi^-\pi^- X$ with $190~\mathrm{GeV}$ pions
\cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.

The interpretation of the two states differs. The $\pi_1(1400)$ lies well below the
predicted hybrid mass and is therefore an unlikely hybrid; a four-quark
interpretation is more natural. The $\pi_1(1600)$, by contrast, sits close to the
predictions and decays into a pair of $P$-wave and $S$-wave mesons ($b_1\pi$ and
$f_1\pi$), exactly as expected for a hybrid, so it behaves like a genuine hybrid
candidate. The search for hybrid mesons is still in its infancy: the remaining
members of the hybrid nonets must be identified, and progress is expected from new
facilities such as GlueX at Jefferson Laboratory, which photoproduces mesons off
nucleons with linearly polarized photons \cite[Ch.~11]{Amsler2018}.
